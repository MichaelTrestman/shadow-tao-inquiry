An investigative blog and scripting tutorial. All scripts referenced are available in the finney-investigation/ directory of this repository.

Why shadow whales are a potential indicator of ‘ecosystem health’: The 51% Problem

Every proof-of-stake network lives or dies by one question: can we trust that the largest holders are acting in good faith?

In Bittensor, the question is sharper than usual. TAO governs emission weights through the root network. Large holders who stake to validators shape which validators earn weight, which shapes what the network optimizes for. As on-chain governance matures — proposals, signaling mechanisms, vote-weighted parameters — the ownership distribution becomes the political map of the protocol itself.

The nightmare scenario isn’t a dramatic exploit. It’s quieter: a single entity accumulates a large, undetected TAO position across multiple wallets that look independent on-chain, waits for a consequential governance moment, and deploys. No warning, no public identity, no accountability. You cannot verify it won’t happen because you cannot verify who holds what.

This is the shadow whale problem: you cannot rule out a coordinated governance attack from dormant wallets because you don’t know who controls them.

The goal of this investigation is to make that uncertainty legible. Not to accuse anyone — the data we’re working with cannot establish intent — but to map the frontier between what can and cannot be known on the Finney chain, and to build the tooling to maintain that map over time.

The Toolkit

Everything in this investigation runs against the Bittensor Python SDK (bittensor) and the Finney public and archive nodes via the substrate RPC. No private APIs, no indexer subscriptions, no scrapers. Just the chain.

import bittensor as bt

# Public node — current state
sub = bt.Subtensor(network="finney")

# Archive node — full block history
sub_archive = bt.Subtensor(network="archive")
# or: bt.Subtensor(network="wss://archive.chain.opentensor.ai")

The substrate interface under the hood is async_substrate_interface, which wraps standard Substrate JSON-RPC methods. Storage maps are queried with sub.substrate.query_map(module, storage_function) and single values with sub.substrate.query(module, storage_function, params). Historical queries use block_hash parameters: every RPC method accepts a block hash, and the archive node keeps the full state at every block.

Phase 1: Enumerating the Chain

The first step is getting a complete picture of who holds what. Substrate chains store all account balances in System.Account — a storage map keyed by AccountId (32 bytes, encoded as SS58). You can stream the entire map in one call:

# enumerate_wallets.py — core loop
for key, data in sub.substrate.query_map("System", "Account"):
    free_tao = data.value["data"]["free"] / 1_000_000_000  # rao to TAO
    nonce    = data.value["nonce"]
    # key is an AccountId — encode to SS58 for human-readable form
    ss58 = ss58_encode(bytes(key[0]).hex(), ss58_format=42)

What nonce tells us. In Substrate, every account has a nonce — a counter that increments each time that account signs and submits an extrinsic (transaction). Nonce = 0 means the account has never originated a transaction. It may have received TAO, but it has never sent anything, staked anything, or interacted with any pallet as a sender.

Alongside the accounts, we pull the staking ledger:

# Who has any stake at all
for key, _ in sub.substrate.query_map("SubtensorModule", "StakingColdkeys"):
    staking_coldkeys.add(ss58_encode(bytes(key[0]).hex(), 42))

And the chain totals for supply accounting:

liquid_supply = sub.substrate.query("Balances", "TotalIssuance").value / 1e9
total_stake   = sub.substrate.query("SubtensorModule", "TotalStake").value / 1e9

Staked TAO is not included in TotalIssuance. When TAO is staked on Bittensor, it moves out of the Balances pallet entirely — Currency::withdraw removes it from the free balance, and it is tracked separately in SubtensorModule. This is confirmed by the near-exact match between TotalIssuance and the sum of all individual free balances (~955 TAO difference, attributable to reserved/frozen balances).

This partitioning has a direct implication for our investigation: if a wallet has a non-zero free balance and nonce = 0, its TAO cannot have come from staking rewards. Staking rewards go into the SubtensorModule stake ledger, not into free balance. To convert staked TAO to free balance requires an unstaking transaction — which would increment the nonce. A nonce-0 wallet with free balance received that balance via a direct Balances.Transfer from another account. Full stop.

Phase 2: Defining Shadow Wallets

With the full account set loaded, we classify:

# analyze_wallets.py — classification
for ss58, free_tao, nonce in accounts:
    has_stake = ss58 in staking_coldkeys
    if nonce == 0 and not has_stake and free_tao > 1.0:
        shadow_wallets.append(...)

A shadow wallet meets all three conditions:

  1. Nonce = 0 — has never sent a transaction
  2. No stake — not in StakingColdkeys
  3. Free balance > 1 TAO — above the dust threshold

This definition has a real limitation: nonce = 0 is consistent with intentional cold storage. Someone can generate a key offline, send TAO to it, and hold it indefinitely without ever signing. For individual wallets, that’s a completely reasonable explanation. The investigation becomes interesting when you look at the distribution collectively and trace the funding history.

Sunny Whales vs. Shadow Whales

Not all large holders are opaque. Bittensor has real sunny whales — large holders who are publicly attributable. Understanding them establishes the baseline that makes shadow wallets anomalous.

Sources of attribution

On-chain identity is the most direct: any coldkey can register a name, URL, GitHub, Discord, and Twitter via SubtensorModule.IdentitiesV2. Querying it is straightforward:

# identity_lookup.py
result = sub.substrate.query("SubtensorModule", "IdentitiesV2", [ss58])
if result and result.value:
    name = result.value.get("name", "")

Registering an identity requires signing a transaction. By definition, no shadow wallet can have on-chain identity — it would immediately disqualify them (nonce would be > 0).

Validator and delegate identity. Every validator registers a hotkey and associates it with a coldkey. Named validators can register delegate identities — human-readable names attached to their hotkey. Query all delegates:

delegates = sub.get_delegates()
# Each DelegateInfo has: hotkey_ss58, owner_ss58 (coldkey), validator_permits, etc.
delegate_identities = sub.get_delegate_identities()  # queries IdentitiesV2 for all delegates

Subnet ownership. Every subnet has an owner coldkey, queryable from get_all_subnets_info(). Subnet owners frequently self-identify through websites, GitHub repositories, and community channels, even if they haven’t registered on-chain identity.

Off-chain attribution. Some wallets are attributable through public disclosure: a founder sharing their address in Discord, an exchange publishing cold wallet addresses for audit purposes, or community-maintained labels on explorers like taostats.io.

Known sunny whales

Binance (5Hd2ze5ug8n1bo3UCAcQsf66VNjKqGos8u6apNfzcU86pg4N) is the largest single TAO holder by free balance with 868,020 TAO. It is an exchange custody cold wallet. Binance has on-chain identity registered (visible on taostats), making it the clearest sunny whale: large, known, and traceable. Exchange cold wallets are identifiable as sunny for behavioral reasons too — they grow and shrink as customers deposit and withdraw, and they’re publicly documented. This is categorically different from the monotonically growing shadow wallets examined below.

Jacob Robert Steeves (Const), Bittensor co-founder, has publicly shared his coldkey 5GH2aUTMRUh1RprCgH4x3tRyCaKeUi5BfmYCfs1NARA8R54n in community channels. In the free-balance ranking, this wallet appears at position #66 with 3,270 TAO free balance — a number that would be misleading without context. This is his free balance only in his publicly known wallet. Const reportedly ranks near #2 on taostats by total holdings; the discrepancy is because the vast majority of his TAO is staked. His position at #66 in the free-balance ranking is a feature of how the data is organized, not a finding about his total wealth. The small free balance is itself informative: Const is actively participating in the network (his stake is deployed), not sitting on a liquid cold hoard.

Kraken validates on the Finney network, which means their hotkey is registered on-chain. The path from hotkey → DelegateInfo → owner_ss58 (coldkey) → balance is queryable. Registered validators with public brand identities represent the highest-confidence sunny whales: their stake, activity, and identity all corroborate each other.

The Liquidity Asymmetry Argument

The free-balance framing surfaces a structural dynamic that matters more than the headline number alone.

Shadow whales dominating the liquid supply is a different kind of threat than dominating total holdings. The distinction hinges on speed of deployment.

Sunny whales are locked in. Const, OTF, and active validators hold most of their TAO as staked balance — it is deployed in the network earning emissions and directing validator weights. To convert staked TAO to deployable capital, they must submit unstaking transactions. Those transactions are on-chain, time-delayed (there is an unstaking period), and visible in the mempool before they settle. A sudden large unstaking event would be detectable — and would itself signal that something consequential is being prepared.

Shadow whales are already liquid. Their holdings are entirely free balance. No prior on-chain action is required to deploy them. In a governance dispute — a token vote, a parameter change, a hostile validator election — shadow whales could act in the same block the vote opens. No warning, no preparatory on-chain footprint.

This reframes the 51% problem from the introduction. The nightmare scenario isn’t just about who holds the most TAO in aggregate. It is about who can act fastest with least warning. If shadow wallets together control a large fraction of immediately deployable TAO, they hold a structural first-mover advantage in any sudden governance contest, regardless of whether they are numerically outnumbered by staked holders in total supply.

The 31.6% shadow wallet figure — over 1,075,000 TAO in liquid, nonce-0 addresses — should be read in this light. It is not just a curiosity about cold storage habits. It is a measure of how much voting power could materialize with zero prior on-chain signal.

Phase 5: The Funding Investigation

Knowing when each wallet was first funded is useful. Knowing who funded it is the prize.

find_first_transfer.py uses binary search against the archive node to narrow down the exact first-funded block for each wallet, then reads the block’s events to find the sending address:

def find_first_nonzero_block(ss58, lo, hi):
    """Binary search: return smallest block where free_balance > 0."""
    while lo < hi - 1:
        mid = (lo + hi) // 2
        bh  = sub.substrate.get_block_hash(block_id=mid)
        bal = get_free_balance(ss58, bh)
        if bal > 0:
            hi = mid
        else:
            lo = mid
    return hi

This is efficient — it takes ~17 RPC calls to narrow a 1,000,000-block range to a single block.

First-funded blocks confirmed for all 10 wallets: the binary search worked. Exact block numbers are in first_transfers.json.

The event lookup required three debugging iterations. This is documented here because it demonstrates a real pitfall when reading Substrate events via the bittensor SDK — the kind of thing that isn’t obvious from the docs.

Bug v1: The original script accessed events as ev.value["event"]["module_id"]. This was wrong — get_events() in this version of bittensor returns plain dicts with top-level fields: ev["module_id"], ev["event_id"], ev["attributes"].

Bug v2: After fixing the access pattern, the script tried to decode attributes["to"] with decode_account_id(), assuming the field would be raw bytes. Also wrong — the RPC layer already decodes AccountId fields to SS58 strings. attributes["from"] and attributes["to"] are ready to use directly.

v3 (current find_first_transfer.py): Uses ev["module_id"] and attrs["to"] directly. The complete bug history is in the script’s docstring.

# v1 — wrong nesting
module = ev.value["event"]["module_id"]   # AttributeError

# v2 — unnecessary decode
to_addr = decode_account_id(attrs["to"])  # TypeError: string without encoding

# v3 — correct
module  = ev["module_id"]
to_addr = attrs["to"]                      # already an SS58 string

Phase 6: Known Holders — Validators and Subnet Owners

known_holders.py enumerates every address that is either (a) registered in IdentitiesV2, (b) a delegate validator coldkey, or (c) a subnet owner coldkey — then fetches free balance and TotalColdkeyStake for each. This builds the population of “known” TAO holders against which shadow wallets can be compared.

The overlap is near-total: 124 of 129 subnet owners are also validators. Subnet ownership and validation are effectively the same population.

Patterns and What They Suggest

With sender attribution now in hand, the pattern picture is much clearer.

Two funders, six wallets. 5EiXej3... made the first deposits into wallets 1, 3, and 5. 5DunDrF... made the first deposits into wallets 2, 6, and 7. These are the two dominant players in the shadow whale ecosystem’s creation — at least at the founding-deposit level. The four remaining wallets each have a distinct first-deposit sender.

Wallet 7’s first deposit was 9 mechanical tranches. 5DunDrF... sent exactly 9 transfers of 996.517 TAO each — 8,968.65 TAO total — into wallet 7 in the same block (7,066,018). Nine transfers of identical amounts in a single block is automated behavior, not a human clicking “send.” This is a script methodically batching a large position into a cold address.

Wallets 6 and 7 were funded fourteen hours apart. 5Epz8SQ6... (wallet 6, first deposit at block 7,061,941) and 5GVKorR7... (wallet 7, first deposit at block 7,066,018) are 4,077 blocks apart — roughly 14 hours — and share the same initial funder. This is confirmed, not inferred.

The rank-#4 wallet seeded wallet #10. 5FqBL928... — the extremely active free-balance holder at position #4 in our ranking, with nonce 181,845 — sent 0.025 TAO to 5CXHJRRk... at block 3,766,862. The 0.025 TAO amount is consistent with a wallet “dusting” — sending a tiny amount to activate or label a cold address. Wallet #10 subsequently received 9,800 TAO and has been static since block 4M. The connection between the most active address in the top 10 and one of the shadow wallets is notable.

Wallets 2, 3, and 4 grew at similar rates. Between block 7M and the analysis date (~103 days): - 5ChHTBka...: +44,632 TAO - 5Dhf6Wgq...: +46,988 TAO - 5GUkyA37...: +43,125 TAO

Similar growth rates over the same period suggest similar inbound transfer schedules — possibly from shared source addresses, or addresses owned by the same entity.

The 12,003 TAO cluster. Four wallets at identical balances. One entity, four addresses. This is the clearest evidence of deliberate multi-wallet strategy in the dataset.

Wallet #10 is different. 5CXHJRRk... received 9,800 TAO before dTAO (block ~3.8M) and has not been touched since. Eighteen months of total inactivity on a round-number position is consistent with genuine cold storage: a one-time transfer, a secured key, indefinite hold. This is the profile of a cold storage wallet that belongs in the discussion but isn’t part of the active-accumulation phenomenon.

The cold storage interpretation. Nonce = 0 is architecturally consistent with intentional cold storage — a key generated offline, funded once, and held. This is legitimate practice. For static wallets like #10, it is probably the right explanation. For the nine actively growing wallets, it strains: cold storage addresses are funded once and left alone. Sending ongoing transfers to the same nonce-0 address over months is unusual — it reduces anonymity (the address is known) without providing the operational utility of an active wallet. The more natural explanation for ongoing accumulation into nonce-0 addresses is that these are receive-only addresses held by an entity that has no need to sign outgoing transactions from them: a custodian, a long-term accumulator, or someone building a position they don’t intend to deploy soon.

Childkey Investigation

Bittensor supports childkey delegation — a hotkey can designate child hotkeys that inherit some of its network permissions. check_childkeys.py checked whether any shadow whale addresses appear as parent or child in the childkey registry:

# check_childkeys.py
for parent_hk, children in sub.substrate.query_map("SubtensorModule", "ChildKeys"):
    for child_hk, proportion in children:
        # check if any shadow address appears as parent or child

Result: zero matches. None of the top shadow wallet addresses appear in any childkey relationship. This result is largely expected — childkey maps store hotkey-to-hotkey relationships, and shadow wallets are coldkeys; the two registries don’t intersect by design. The check would only surface an anomaly if someone had reused the same address as both a hotkey and a coldkey, which would itself be a notable finding. No such overlap exists here.

Open Research Questions

1. Who made the first deposits? Resolved. find_first_transfer.py identified the first-deposit sender for all 10 wallets. Two addresses (5EiXej3... and 5DunDrF...) account for 6 of 10 first deposits. Full attribution is in first_transfers.json.

2. What are the two dominant sender addresses? 5EiXej3AwjKqb9mjQAf29JG5HJf9Dwtt8CjvDqA8biprWTiN and 5DunDrFV6BPbzsgLZUtMQtCq7w9fcUEbM59BUwx5E7N9Bn5E are the highest-priority investigation targets. Are they validators? Exchange withdrawal addresses? Do they have on-chain identity? What is their full outbound transfer history — did they fund other wallets beyond the ones we’re tracking?

3. Does a single entity control both 5EiXej3 and 5DunDrF? Together they seeded 6 of the top 10 shadow wallets. If the same controller operates both, then a single entity is responsible for the majority of the top shadow whale positions. Tracing these addresses backward — who funded them, when, and whether they share a common upstream source — is the critical next question.

4. Where does the ongoing TAO come from? Each of the nine growing wallets has received multiple transfers beyond the first deposit. The total balances are 10–100× the initial deposits. Tracing the full inbound transfer history requires dense block scanning or an indexed event store (the taoapp ClickHouse database is a candidate for this).

5. What is 5FqBL928...? This wallet (rank #4 by free balance, nonce 181,845) seeded the pre-dTAO shadow wallet 5CXHJRRk... with 0.025 TAO at block 3.8M. With nonce ~182k it is one of the most active addresses on the chain. Its identity is unknown — it does not appear in known_holders_report.txt. What is it?

6. Are 5FqBL928… and 5H9brHhM… shadow wallets? Resolved: No. Nonce 181,845 and 111 respectively. Neither qualifies. See nonce_check.json.

7. Correlation with protocol events. Does the appearance of new shadow wallets (July 2025, October 2025, December 2025, February 2026) correlate with specific network events — subnet launches, price movements, governance milestones?

On Methodology and Fairness

Holding TAO in a nonce-0 cold wallet is completely legal and architecturally sound. The analysis makes no claims about intent. Cold storage, long-term investment, OTC settlement, estate planning — all of these are consistent with what the on-chain data shows.

The point of this investigation is not accusation but epistemic mapping: establishing what can and cannot be known about who holds Bittensor, and building tools that make that knowledge updatable over time. A community that can ask and partially answer “who are the largest holders and what are their interests?” is in a much stronger governance position than one that cannot.

The shadow whale problem is ultimately a transparency design question. The current chain gives large holders total anonymity by default. That’s a feature for individual privacy, but a bug for collective governance. The healthiest path forward probably isn’t forced disclosure — it’s better tooling for voluntary attribution and governance mechanisms that weight active, legible participation over passive, anonymous accumulation.
